# The Numbers That Actually Matter When You Scale

Most store owners learn one number first: the ROAS shown inside the ads manager. It feels like the truth. You spent R1,000, the dashboard says 3x, so you made R3,000. Simple. The problem is that this number quietly stops being trustworthy at exactly the moment it matters most: when you start to scale. This deep-dive is about the handful of numbers that do not lie, and how to use them to make a confident scaling decision instead of a hopeful one.

## Why the in-platform ROAS number drifts from reality

The ROAS you see inside the ad platform is built from what the pixel can observe. The pixel is a piece of tracking code that tries to connect a sale back to an ad. When everything happens in one browser session, it works well. But real buyers do not behave that way. They click an ad on the train, get distracted, come back that evening on a different device, ask a question on WhatsApp, then buy two days later. Every one of those hops weakens the pixel's ability to join the click to the sale.

So the platform credits some sales to the wrong ad, misses others completely, and counts a few twice. On a small budget the errors are small. As you scale and traffic gets messier, the gap widens. You end up optimising against a number that is partly fiction.

## Blended MER: the number that cannot hide

Blended MER, or marketing efficiency ratio, is the antidote. It is almost insultingly simple. Take all the revenue your store actually made in a period, from your store's own back office, not the ad platform. Divide it by everything you spent on marketing in that same period. That ratio is your blended MER.

If your store did R50,000 in real revenue this week and you spent R20,000 across all ads, your blended MER is 2.5. It does not care which ad got credit. It does not care about cookies or browsers or attribution windows. It answers the only question that pays your bills: for every rand I put into marketing, how many rand came back into the business?

The discipline is to make blended MER your headline number and demote the in-platform ROAS to a supporting clue. The platform number is still useful for comparing one ad against another inside the same account on the same day. It is just not the scoreboard.

## Breakeven: know it before you spend a cent

You cannot judge any ROAS or MER number until you know your breakeven point. Breakeven is the ratio at which you make exactly zero profit. Below it you lose money on every sale. Above it you make money.

To find it, work out your true margin on an average order. Start with the selling price. Subtract the product cost, the shipping you actually pay, the payment processing fee, and the slice of VAT that is not yours to keep. What remains is your contribution margin. Your breakeven ROAS is simply one divided by that margin expressed as a fraction.

A quick example. Say on a typical order you keep 45 cents of every rand after product, shipping, fees, and VAT. Your margin is 0.45. One divided by 0.45 is about 2.2. That means you need a blended MER of roughly 2.2 just to stand still. A 2.0 that looked fine in the dashboard is actually quietly bleeding you. A 3.0 is healthy. Without knowing that 2.2, you are flying blind.

## Why the pixel under-reports, and the cheap fix

The single biggest reason the pixel misses sales for stores like ours is the WhatsApp detour. An ad sends someone to chat, the sale gets closed in conversation, and the buyer checks out later in a fresh browser with no memory of the original ad click. The platform sees a sale appear from nowhere and cannot connect it to the ad that paid for it.

The fix is not to abandon WhatsApp, which is often your best closer. The fix is to feed the platform the missing information through a server-side connection, so a sale that happened off to the side still gets matched back to the click that started it. When that link is restored, two good things happen. Your reported numbers move closer to your true blended MER, and more importantly the platform's algorithm finally learns what a real buyer looks like, so it goes and finds more of them. Better tracking is not just cleaner reporting. It is a direct upgrade to how well the machine spends your money.

## The three-signal scaling check

When you are deciding whether to push more budget into something, do not rely on a single number on a single day. Use three signals together.

First, blended MER over a rolling seven days, not one day. One day is noise. A week is a trend. If your seven-day blended MER is comfortably above breakeven, you have room.

Second, the direction of travel. Is the MER holding steady as you have added budget over the last few days, or is it sliding every time you spend more? A winner holds its efficiency as it grows. A fragile setup loses efficiency the moment you feed it.

Third, the volume of conversions the platform is actually getting. An ad set needs a steady flow of sales each week to stay out of the learning phase, where performance is jumpy and unreliable. If you spread a small budget across many ad sets, every one of them starves and none of them ever learns. It is almost always better to concentrate budget into fewer, well-fed ad sets than to scatter it.

Only when all three signals line up, healthy MER, stable under added budget, and enough conversions to learn, do you scale with confidence. If one is off, you fix that first.

## The costs South African sellers forget

A few local realities quietly eat margins and make a dashboard number look better than your bank balance.

VAT is the big one. If you are registered, a chunk of every sale is not your money, and there is VAT on your ad spend too. Build both into your margin before you calculate breakeven, or you will scale a business that looks profitable on screen and is not.

Returns and failed deliveries are the second. A sale that comes back is not a sale, but the ad spend that won it is gone for good. Track your real return rate and fold it into your margin.

Payment fees, currency costs on imported stock, and the cost of the time you spend on support all belong in the same honest accounting. The goal is one true margin number you trust, because every scaling decision rests on it.

## A simple weekly routine

You do not need a complex dashboard to run this well. Once a week, pull your real revenue from your store's back office and your total marketing spend from the ad platform. Divide one by the other for your blended MER. Compare it to your breakeven. Look at whether it held up as you spent more. Then make one decision: push the winners, hold the steady ones, and cut what is clearly below breakeven and not improving.

Do that every week, judge by blended MER, respect your breakeven, and fix your tracking so the platform can see your real sales. That is the difference between scaling on hope and scaling on the numbers that actually matter.
