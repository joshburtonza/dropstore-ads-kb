Alex: Welcome back. Episode nine, and this one is my favourite topic. Hooks. The thing that actually stops someone scrolling.

Sam: It is the whole game, honestly. You can have the best product on the shelf and the cheapest supplier, and if nobody stops scrolling, none of it matters.

Alex: Right. So let me start with the myth, because I hear it constantly. People think paid ads are about secret settings. Find the magic button in the ad manager and the platform rewards you.

Sam: And that is mostly wrong. The platform is not a puzzle to beat. It has one goal: show people stuff they want to engage with so they stay on the app. So when your ad gets a lot of clicks, the platform reads that as, this is relevant, people like it.

Alex: And then it charges you less.

Sam: Over time, yes. High click-through, which is just the share of people who see your ad and click it, that drives your costs down. Low engagement pushes them up. So the lever is not a setting. The lever is making something people genuinely want to engage with.

Alex: Which is a copywriting and creative problem.

Sam: Exactly. And the good news is you can get good at it.

Alex: So let me tee up the idea that changed how I write ads. Market awareness. Most people scrolling are not ready to buy.

Sam: Picture a pyramid. At the very top, a thin little band of people are actively shopping for exactly what you sell right now. Below them, a bigger group knows they have a problem but has not linked it to your product. And at the bottom, the biggest group, people who do not even know they have a problem yet.

Alex: And the mistake is writing every ad for that thin band at the top.

Sam: Every single ad screaming buy now, fifty percent off. That only works on the few who were already going to buy. Everyone else scrolls past, because you are asking for a decision they are nowhere near.

Alex: So a good ad warms people up instead.

Sam: It moves someone one step up the pyramid. You are not closing the sale in the ad. You are taking someone problem-aware and showing them the problem clearly. The sale comes later, on the page, on the second or third time they see you.

Alex: Okay. So if the ad is not closing the sale, what is it actually doing? You always say an ad has two jobs.

Sam: Two jobs, at the same time. Job one, deliver real value. Teach them something, a tip, a piece of news, a genuine reason they are better off for having seen it. Job two, earn the click with curiosity. A little open loop, a question they cannot help wanting answered.

Alex: And the trap is doing one without the other.

Sam: Pure curiosity with nothing behind it is empty clickbait. You will not believe what happened next. They click, they feel cheated, they bounce, and the platform learns your ad disappoints people. Pure value with no curiosity is honest and completely invisible. Nobody stops.

Alex: So you want the intersection.

Sam: Curiosity that opens a loop, and a payoff that actually closes it on the other side. And the honesty rule is simple. Never promise something the click does not deliver. The curiosity has to pay off with real value, every time.

Alex: Let's get concrete. When you break down ads that work, what shows up?

Sam: Three things together. A scroll-stopping image, something that breaks the rhythm of the feed. A curiosity headline that opens the loop. And a big, specific benefit. Not improve your life. Something you can picture, with a detail attached.

Alex: And you need all three.

Sam: Drop any one and it weakens. Curiosity without benefit pulls the wrong people who never buy. Benefit without curiosity gets scrolled past. The image alone gets a glance and nothing.

Alex: Let's talk about the image idea more, because this is the one people resist. Make ads that do not look like ads.

Sam: People have been sold to their whole lives. They have a reflex. The instant something looks like an advert, the brain files it under ignore and the thumb keeps moving. The studio shot, the logo in the corner, the perfect model. All of it says, this is an ad, skip.

Alex: So you blend in.

Sam: Look like the rest of the feed. A raw photo that looks shot on a phone. A text message screenshot. A behind-the-scenes look, you packing orders in your garage in Durban. A clear before-and-after, where the contrast itself is the story.

Alex: Not fake, though.

Sam: Never fake. It is presenting something true in the format people actually engage with, instead of the format their brain has learned to ignore.

Alex: Okay, the big one. The first line.

Sam: If anyone takes one thing from this episode, take this. The most overlooked, highest-leverage part of any ad is the opening line. There is an old direct-response idea, the slippery slide. The only job of line one is to get line two read. Line two exists to get line three read. All the way down.

Alex: And people spend all their effort on the offer at the bottom.

Sam: Which almost nobody reaches if the top does not pull them in. Write your first line like it is the only thing that matters, because for most readers it is the only thing they will ever see.

Alex: How do you write a good one?

Sam: Short. Open a small gap they want closed. Speak to one person about something they care about. And do not try to say everything at once. Just earn the next line.

Alex: Which leads into readability. Write the way you talk.

Sam: Most of your readers are on a phone, half distracted. Short words, short lines, simple sentences. Aim for the reading level of a nine or ten year old. Not because they are not smart, but because easy reading is fast reading, and fast is what survives a noisy feed.

Alex: And long copy?

Sam: Long can beat short, but only if it stays interesting the whole way. Keep the slide slippery. The moment it gets boring they are gone, and a long boring ad just gives them more chances to leave. Length is fine. Dull is fatal.

Alex: And write to one person.

Sam: Use you. Not customers, not everyone. A real human reads it alone. It should feel written for them alone.

Alex: Then specificity. Why do numbers matter so much?

Sam: Vague claims slide off. We have all been told a thousand times something is amazing and we believe none of it. But specifics feel true. Our cleaner works well, versus, it lifts braai grease off a stainless pan in about forty seconds, no scrubbing. The second one you can picture.

Alex: With honesty rules.

Sam: Always. Any number is an illustration, not a guarantee. Never invent a statistic to sound credible. Real specifics beat fake ones, and fake ones eventually cost you trust and refunds. Be concrete and be true at the same time.

Alex: Two last dials before we wrap.

Sam: Lead with the upside, not fear. Carry your phone without a second thought, rather than stop your screen cracking. For most products in most South African feeds, the positive version pulls better and leaves the reader feeling good about you. And keep the call to action soft. Learn more, not buy now. They have not committed yet. Match where their head actually is.

Alex: So give me the routine. What does someone do this week?

Sam: Once a week, sit down and write five opening lines for one product. Not five ads, five first lines. For each one, write the big specific benefit the click will pay off. If you cannot name a real benefit, it is empty curiosity, bin it.

Alex: Then?

Sam: Pick the two strongest. Pair each with a native-style image, raw phone photo, before-and-after, text-message look. No studio polish. Run the two against each other on a small budget, a few hundred rand. Let click-through tell you which hook the market responds to, not which one you liked best. Keep the winner, write three new challengers next week, and never let the winner run unchallenged too long, because hooks wear out as people keep seeing them.

Alex: The feed is the judge.

Sam: The feed is the judge. Not you. Write five hooks, name the benefit, go native, test two, let the thumb decide.

Alex: That is the work. Thanks everyone, see you next episode.
