# Hooks That Stop the Scroll

A person in Cape Town is lying in bed at 11pm, thumb moving up the screen. A friend's braai photo. A news clip. A meme. Your ad. Another meme. They are not reading. They are scanning, at speed, deciding in a fraction of a second whether each thing is worth slowing down for. Your job is to be the one thing that makes the thumb stop.

That moment is where most ad money is won or lost. Not in the targeting settings. Not in the bid strategy. In the first image and the first line. This episode is about how to earn that pause honestly, using psychology and copywriting that work because they respect the reader, not because they trick them.

## The edge is not the algorithm

There is a popular belief that paid ads are a game of secret settings. Find the right hidden toggle, the right audience hack, and the platform rewards you. That belief is mostly wrong, and chasing it will cost you money.

The platform is not trying to be beaten. It is trying to do one thing: show people content they want to engage with, so they stay on the app longer. When your ad gets a high click-through rate, which is the share of people who see it and actually click, you are telling the platform that your ad is the kind of content people want. The platform reads that as relevance, and over time it charges you less to reach more people. Low engagement signals the opposite, and your costs creep up.

So the real lever is not a setting. It is making something people genuinely want to engage with. That is a creative and copywriting problem, and one you can actually get good at.

## Most people are not ready to buy yet

Here is the idea that changes how you write ads. At any moment, only a small slice of the market is ready to buy right now. Picture a pyramid. At the very top, a thin band of people are actively shopping for exactly what you sell. Below them, a bigger group knows they have a problem but has not connected it to your product. Below that, the largest group does not even know they have a problem yet.

This is called market awareness. The mistake most store owners make is writing every ad as if everyone is in that thin band at the top. "Buy now, fifty percent off." That message only lands with the few who were already going to buy. Everyone else scrolls past, because you are asking for a decision they are nowhere near ready to make.

Good ads do something different. They warm people up. They take someone who is problem-aware and show them the problem clearly, then point at a solution. They take someone who is unaware and make them feel the problem for the first time. You are not closing a sale in the ad. You are moving someone one step up the pyramid. The sale comes later, on the page, in the follow-up, in the second or third time they see you.

## Your ad has two jobs

Every ad you run has to do two things at once, and most ads only do one.

The first job is to deliver real value. A useful insight, a piece of news, a tip, a genuine reason the reader is better off for having seen it. If your ad teaches a Joburg homeowner one thing about keeping their geyser from bursting in winter, you have given value even before they click.

The second job is to earn the click with curiosity. Value alone, stated flatly, gets ignored. You need a small open loop, a question the reader cannot help wanting answered.

The trap is doing one without the other. Pure curiosity with nothing behind it is empty clickbait. "You will not believe what happened next." It pulls the click, but the reader feels cheated, bounces off your page, and the platform learns that your ad disappoints people. Pure value with no curiosity is honest and invisible. Nobody stops.

The winners live in the intersection. Curiosity that opens a loop, and a payoff that genuinely closes it on the other side. The rule that keeps you honest: never promise something the click does not deliver. The curiosity must be paid off with real value, every time.

## The winning combination

When you break down ads that consistently work, three things show up together.

A scroll-stopping image. Something that interrupts the pattern of the feed. The feed has a rhythm of polished, expected images, and the brain skips anything that matches it. A pattern-interrupt breaks the rhythm. An unexpected angle, a raw photo, something slightly off from what the eye predicted.

A curiosity-driven headline. The line that opens the loop and makes the reader want the rest of the story.

A big, specific benefit. Not vague. Not "improve your life." Something concrete the reader can picture, with detail attached.

Curiosity gets the attention, the benefit gives a reason to act, and the image earns the stop in the first place. Drop any one and the ad weakens. Curiosity without benefit attracts the wrong people who never buy. Benefit without curiosity gets scrolled past. The image without either gets a glance and nothing more.

## Make ads that do not look like ads

People have spent their whole lives being sold to. They have a reflex now. The instant something looks like an advert, the brain files it under "ignore" and the thumb keeps moving. The polished studio shot, the logo in the corner, the perfect model, the obvious sales layout, these all signal "this is an ad," and they trigger the skip.

So the highest-leverage creative move is often to not look like an ad at all. Look like the rest of the feed. Look like content a friend would post.

Here are native styles that work because they blend in. A raw photo that looks shot on a phone, not a studio. A simple text-message screenshot, the kind of thing someone would actually send. A behind-the-scenes look, you packing orders in your garage in Durban, the real version of your business. A clear before-and-after, which works because the contrast itself is the story.

None of this means being fake. It means presenting something true in the format people actually engage with, instead of the format their brain has been trained to ignore.

## The opening line is everything

If you take one thing from this episode, take this. The single most overlooked, highest-leverage part of any ad is the first line.

There is an old idea from direct-response copywriting called the slippery slide. The only job of your first line is to get the second line read. The only job of the second line is to get the third read. And so on, all the way down, the reader sliding through the copy because each line makes stopping feel like missing something.

Most store owners spend all their effort on the offer and the call to action at the bottom, and write the opening line as an afterthought. That is backwards. Almost nobody reaches the bottom if the top does not pull them in. Write your first line last if you have to, but write it like it is the only thing that matters, because for most readers it is the only thing they will ever see.

A strong opener is short. It creates a small gap the reader wants closed. It speaks directly to one person about something they care about. And it never tries to say everything at once. It just earns the next line.

## Write the way you talk

Most of your readers are on a phone, half-distracted, reading in between other things. Dense, clever, formal writing dies in that environment. The copy that works reads like a person talking to you.

Short words. Short lines. Simple sentences. Aim for the reading level of a nine or ten year old, not because your customers are not smart, but because easy reading is fast reading, and fast reading is what survives a noisy feed. Clever sentences make the reader work, and a reader who has to work will simply leave.

This does not mean short ads always win. Long copy can outperform short copy, but only on one condition: it has to stay interesting the whole way down. Every line has to keep the slide slippery. The moment it gets boring, the reader is gone, and a long boring ad just gives them more chances to leave. Length is fine. Dull is fatal.

One more habit. Write to one person, not a crowd. Use "you," not "customers" or "everyone." A real human reads your ad alone, and it should feel like it was written for them alone.

## Specificity is how you become believable

Vague claims slide off people. We have all been told a thousand times that something is "amazing" or "life-changing," and we have learned to believe none of it. Specific details, on the other hand, feel true. The brain treats precision as evidence.

Compare "our cleaner works really well" with "it lifts braai grease off a stainless pan in about forty seconds, no scrubbing." The second one you can picture. It has a number, a surface, a timeframe. It sounds like someone who actually used the thing, because vague claims are what people make up and specific ones are what people observe.

A few honesty rules here, because specificity is powerful and power gets abused. Any number you use to illustrate a point is an example, not a guarantee. If you say a product saves time or money, you are describing what is possible for a real user, not promising a result for every buyer. Never invent a statistic to sound credible. Real specifics beat fake ones, and fake ones eventually cost you trust, refunds, and your standing with the platform. Be concrete and be true at the same time.

## Lead with the upside, and ask gently

Two final dials worth setting correctly.

First, lead with a positive benefit rather than fear. You can frame the same product as "stop your phone screen from cracking again" or as "carry your phone without a second thought." Fear works sometimes, but for most products, in most South African feeds, the positive version tends to pull better and leaves the reader feeling good about you rather than anxious. Show them the better version of their day, not just the disaster you are saving them from.

Second, keep the call to action soft. The reader has not committed to anything yet. A hard "Buy now" asks for a decision they are not ready to make, especially the problem-aware and unaware people you are warming up. A gentle "Learn more" matches where their head actually is. It says, come have a look, no pressure. You can ask for the sale later, on the page, once they have chosen to step closer.

## Your weekly hook routine

Knowing this and doing it are different things. Here is a simple routine to turn it into a habit.

Once a week, sit down and write five new opening lines for one product. Not five ads. Five first lines. Force yourself to make each one open a different loop or hit a different awareness level.

For each of the five, write the big specific benefit that the click will pay off. If you cannot name a concrete benefit, the hook is empty curiosity, so bin it.

Pick the two strongest. Pair each with a native-style image: a raw phone photo, a before-and-after, a text-message look, a behind-the-scenes shot. No studio polish.

Run the two against each other on a small budget. Let click-through rate tell you which hook the market actually responds to, not which one you liked best. The feed is the judge, not you.

Keep the winner, write three new challengers next week, and never let the winner run unchallenged for long. Hooks wear out as people see them again and again, so you are always feeding the top of the funnel with something fresh.

## The hook checklist

Before any hook goes live, run it through this:

- Does the first line make me want to read the second? If not, rewrite it.
- Is there genuine value here, something the reader is better off knowing, not just a tease?
- Is there a big, specific benefit, with a real detail or number, not a vague promise?
- Does the curiosity get honestly paid off after the click, with no misleading promise?
- Could this pass as native content, or does it scream "advert" and trigger the skip?
- Am I writing to one person, using "you," in words a child could read?
- Did I lead with the upside, and keep the call to action soft, like "Learn more"?

If you can tick all seven, you have a hook worth a few hundred rand to test. The thumb is moving. Give it a real reason to stop.
