Alex: Right, episode seven. We are getting into the psychology of ads that actually sell. And I want to start with the thing most store owners believe, which is that a winning ad is basically luck.

Sam: It is the slot machine model. You film ten things, you boost all of them, one randomly takes off, and you have no clue why it worked.

Alex: And because you do not know why, you cannot do it again. So you are back at the slot machine next month.

Sam: Right. But here is the actual point. Ads that sell are not random. When you take apart the winners, across almost any market, almost any product, you find the same skeleton underneath. Five parts, in order. Once you can see the skeleton, you stop guessing.

Alex: So let us walk the five. Part one is the pattern interrupt.

Sam: The thing that stops the scroll. That is its whole job. Someone is on the couch, thumb moving on autopilot, and you have maybe one to three seconds to break that before they are gone.

Alex: And people get this wrong two ways.

Sam: Two ways, yes. Either they do nothing, like a slow logo intro the brain reads as an advert and skips, or they overcorrect and do something wild and loud that has nothing to do with the product. You stopped the scroll but you grabbed someone who will never buy.

Alex: So what is the move?

Sam: The most underrated interrupt is just calling out exactly who the ad is for. You sell knee braces for runners, your first line is, if your knees ache after every long run, watch this. Every runner with sore knees stops dead. No stunt needed.

Alex: There is a lovely South African version of this. A store selling load-shedding camping lights opens with a kitchen going dark and a voice going, stage six again.

Sam: And every single South African feels that. The scroll stops because the moment is real. Relevant beats wild almost every time.

Alex: Okay, scroll is stopped. Part two, self-recognition. The avatar.

Sam: The viewer needs to feel, right away, that this is for them specifically. And the key idea is that an avatar is a type of person, not a demographic box.

Alex: Spell that out, because people confuse the two constantly.

Sam: Women aged twenty-five to thirty-four is not an avatar. That is a billing setting. An avatar is the new mom who has not slept in four months and feels guilty she snaps at her partner. A person with a life and a worry. You can picture them.

Alex: And why does specific win?

Sam: Because people scroll past general and stop for specific. An ad for everyone is an ad for no one. The second a viewer thinks, that is literally me, you have their attention and a bit of trust, because it feels like you get them.

Alex: Example?

Sam: A postpartum recovery store films in a normal flat, not a studio, tired mom in a faded shirt, third week home with the baby and I still do not feel like myself. A specific mom feels seen. The for-all-women version slides right past her.

Alex: Part three, problem articulation. And this is the one I think is the heart of it.

Sam: It is. People do not buy products, they buy solutions to problems. Nobody wants a drill, they want the shelf up so the lounge looks finished. Your job is to name the real problem better than they could name it themselves.

Alex: And there are flavours of problem.

Sam: Four of them. Pain, an actual frustration. Status, how you are seen. Desire, wanting something better. And convenience, less hassle. Strong ads press on one or two clearly instead of waving at all four.

Alex: The trick I like is naming the exact moment the problem bites.

Sam: Yes. Not your kitchen is messy, but you open the cupboard and three lunchboxes fall on your foot. Specific moments are felt. Vague statements get skimmed. There is a great local one, a store selling boot organisers for bakkies. You load the groceries, hit one pothole on the way home, everything is rolling around the back by the time you reach the gate.

Alex: Anyone who drives South African roads feels that in their body.

Sam: Exactly. Now the convenience problem is real and worth solving.

Alex: Part four, and this is the one people skip. The unique mechanism.

Sam: The specific reason your thing actually works. The question in the viewer's head is, why yours and not the ten other options. The mechanism answers it.

Alex: And if you skip it?

Sam: Then you are just a product at a price. The only lever left is being cheaper, and that is a race you do not want to win. A mechanism can be a feature, a special material, a clever design. Or it can be service, faster local delivery, setup done for you, support in your own time zone.

Alex: Give me the structure.

Sam: Problem, then mechanism as the answer. Everything rolls around your boot. Ours has a non-slip base and dividers that lock to your exact boot shape, so nothing moves no matter how bad the road is. The mechanism is doing the persuading.

Alex: And there was a skincare one.

Sam: A local store against big imported brands. Formulated for our climate, shipped from Joburg, at your door in two to three days, not three weeks from overseas with customs surprises. Real reason to choose them, nothing to do with price.

Alex: Part five. Proof and risk-reversal.

Sam: The viewer wants it now, the last barrier is doubt. Will it work for me, and what if I get burned. Proof is evidence from people who are not you. Testimonials, reviews, real customer clips. We trust other buyers way more than we trust the seller.

Alex: And risk-reversal makes saying yes safe.

Sam: Money-back guarantee, free returns, a warranty. The message is, if it does not work, you lose nothing. And for South African shoppers this matters even more, because stores that take your money and vanish are a real fear. Proof and a guarantee are the difference between a stranger trusting you with their card or closing the tab.

Alex: Now tie it together, because there is a bigger point about targeting.

Sam: This is the bit most people miss. On Meta, the creative is the targeting. Your avatar call-out and your problem are a filter. The right person stops and engages, the wrong person scrolls on, and the algorithm watches who reacts and goes and finds more people exactly like them.

Alex: So fiddling with tiny audience settings while running a weak ad is backwards.

Sam: Completely. A sharp creative with a clear avatar out-targets a vague creative with a perfect audience, every time. Fix the ad and the targeting mostly sorts itself out.

Alex: So let us land the action. What does someone do with this?

Sam: Before any ad goes live, run the five questions. Is there a relevant interrupt in the first three seconds. Would one specific person think this is for me. Did you name a real problem and the moment it hurts. Is there one clear reason your solution works. Is there proof from real customers and a way out with no risk. If any answer is weak, that is where the ad leaks money.

Alex: And make it a weekly habit.

Sam: Thirty minutes a week. Score every ad you plan to run out of five, fix or kill anything under five. Take last week's worst ad, find the one missing part, rewrite just that, test again. And save one ad you admire from any brand, anywhere, and label its five parts.

Alex: Build the muscle of seeing the skeleton.

Sam: Do that for two months and you read ads like a builder reads a house. Structure first, decoration second. That is the whole shift.

Alex: So the takeaway is concrete. Open the next ad you plan to run, score it out of five against those five questions, and fix the lowest part before you spend a rand.

Sam: Score before you spend. Do it on the very next ad in your queue.
