Alex: So this guy has spent over three hundred million dollars on Meta ads and dropped eight hacks. Give me the ones that actually matter.
Sam: Happily, because almost none of it is secret settings. It is how you make creative and what number you judge yourself by. Hack one, volume beats polish.
Alex: Meaning?
Sam: The algorithm is hungry. It needs a steady stream of fresh creative or your ads fatigue, they get tired and your costs climb. You cannot feed that with two beautiful videos. He said if he could only pick one format, he would pick simple static images every time, because he can make way more of them, way faster and cheaper.
Alex: So I do not need a film crew.
Sam: Not even close. Plain native-looking statics often beat expensive video. Block one hour a week, take your winning product, make a batch of fresh simple creatives. Done.
Alex: Hack two.
Sam: The identity-trigger keyword. The algorithm reads your image, copy, offer, even your landing page, to decide who to show it to. So if you want a certain buyer, name them in the ad.
Alex: Give me an example.
Sam: Take your best ad, duplicate it, and drop in a word that names the exact buyer. For new moms, for gym owners, for bakkie owners, whatever your real groups are. It speaks to that person so they feel seen, and it tells the algorithm who to hunt for. Drops your cost per result and opens new pockets of buyers.
Alex: Nice. Hack three.
Sam: Do not ride one winner to death. The amateur move is to find a winner, kill everything else, and ride it until it fatigues and dies. Instead, multiply it. Use AI to write loads of variations of your winner, including versions for different buyers, and let Meta push budget to the ones that work.
Alex: And the zombie thing I saw in there?
Sam: Love that one. Usually a bunch of those ads get no spend. The ones you still believe in, you relaunch together in their own campaign. He says that zombie campaign regularly digs out more winners that would have been left for dead.
Alex: Hack four.
Sam: Do not make your ads look like ads. People hate ads, hundreds of millions installed ad blockers. So making it loud and flashy so it pops is backwards. Make it look like the content people already choose to watch.
Alex: How do I find that?
Sam: Proven organic content makes the best ads. If a post did well naturally, put money behind it. If you have none, make a fresh clean social account, follow all the big pages in your niche, and watch what the algorithm promotes to the top. That is your template.
Alex: Hack five, targeting.
Sam: The creative is the targeting now. Stop stacking ten interests trying to be a sniper. The algorithm reads your ad to decide who to show it to, so the more context you give it, the better it finds your people. Short basic ad, tiny reach. Longer copy that describes exactly who it is for, and it goes broad and finds them.
Alex: So go broad.
Sam: Go broad, let the specific creative do the targeting. Run your old interest-stacked ad against a broad version for a week and compare cost per result. Most people are shocked.
Alex: Hack six.
Sam: Match your ad to your page. Most people spend ninety percent of their effort on the ad and nothing on the page. If the page does not match what they clicked, they lose the scent and bounce. Great click-through, sales in the toilet.
Alex: The fix.
Sam: Take your best performing ad headline and mirror it on your product page, the heading, the sub-heading, the opening line. That alone often lifts conversion fifteen to twenty percent. Meta is the best headline split-tester on earth, so use the winner on your page.
Alex: Hack seven.
Sam: Retarget with a different offer. When someone does not buy, do not just nag them with the same ad. There was a reason, usually that offer was not right for them. Show them a different product. Handle their objections head on. Show proof and reviews. People who already know you are way cheaper to convert.
Alex: And the last one.
Sam: This is the one that separates people who run ads from people who run businesses. Track the metric that matters. Do not protect your return-on-ad-spend percentage. Protect net cash. Look at your blended return across the whole business, find your breakeven, and scale hard toward it, even if the percentage drops. Going from a ten times return on small spend to a five times on big spend makes you far more actual rands.
Alex: Protect the cash, not the percentage.
Sam: And block a few hours a month to look at your own numbers yourself. Not a tool, not your team, you. That confidence is what lets you scale with speed instead of fear.
Alex: Land it.
Sam: Feed the machine native creative, name your buyer in the copy, multiply your winners, go broad, match your page, retarget with a different offer, and judge on net cash. That is running ads like a business.
Alex: One hour of creative a week, one monthly look at the real numbers. Go.
