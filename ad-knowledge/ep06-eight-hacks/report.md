# Eight Hacks From a 300 Million Dollar Meta Advertiser

Someone who has spent more than three hundred million dollars on Meta ads recently shared the eight things he wishes he had known sooner. Most of it is gold, and almost none of it is about secret settings. It is about how you make creative, how you target, and what number you actually judge yourself by. Here is the operator's version, adapted for a store like yours.

## One: volume of creative beats polish, and statics punch above their weight

The modern algorithm is hungry. It needs a steady stream of fresh creative or your ads fatigue, which means they get tired, people stop responding, and your costs climb. You cannot out-produce that with two or three beautifully polished videos. The person who has spent hundreds of millions said that if he had to choose only one format, he would pick simple static images over video, every time, because he can make far more of them, far faster and cheaper.

The lesson for you is freeing. You do not need a studio or a film crew. Plain, native-looking statics often beat expensive video that took weeks to make. So block one hour a week, take your winning product, and make a batch of fresh simple creatives. Consistent volume of decent creative beats the occasional masterpiece.

## Two: the identity-trigger keyword

This one is small and quietly powerful. The algorithm reads everything in your ad, the image, the copy, the offer, even your landing page, to work out who to show it to. So if you want to reach a certain kind of buyer, you have to name them in the creative itself.

Take your best ad, duplicate it, and drop in a word that names the exact buyer or use case. Instead of a generic line, you make it speak to a specific person: for new moms, for gym owners, for bakkie owners, whatever your real buyer groups are. It is not a magic word, it is an identity trigger, and it does two things. It speaks directly to that person so they feel seen, and it tells the algorithm who to go and find. This alone can drop your cost per result and open up pockets of buyers a generic ad would never reach.

## Three: clone your winner into many variations, do not ride one ad to death

Here is the amateur move. You find a winning ad, you turn everything else off, and you ride that one winner as hard as you can. Then it fatigues, it dies, and you have nothing, because you rested everything on one ad.

Instead, when you find a winner, multiply it. Use AI to study that winning ad and write many variations of it, including versions aimed at different kinds of buyer, while keeping what made it work. Put that set into a campaign where Meta controls the budget and let it push spend toward the variations that perform. And here is the clever bit. Often a bunch of those ads get no spend at all. Take the ones you still believe in, the no-spend ads you have real conviction about, and relaunch them together in their own campaign. He calls it a zombie campaign, and he says it regularly squeezes out another batch of winners that would otherwise have been left for dead.

## Four: do not make your ads look like ads

People hate ads. Hundreds of millions of them have installed ad blockers to avoid ads. So the instinct to make your ad loud and flashy so it pops off the feed is exactly backwards. The flashier it looks like an ad, the faster people scroll past.

Do the opposite. Make your creative look like the content people already choose to watch on the platform. Proven organic content makes the best ads, because it has already earned attention without paying for it. If you have a post or a video that did well naturally, put money behind that one. If you do not, here is a trick: make a fresh, clean social account, follow all the big pages and creators in your niche, and let the feed fill up with whatever the algorithm is already promoting. That is your template for the kind of native, non-ad content you should be making and running.

## Five: broad targeting with specific creative

There are two ways people get targeting wrong. The first is trying to be a sniper, stacking interest after interest until they have built one ultra-narrow audience they are sure is perfect. The second is pure yolo, pick a country and blast everyone. The truth sits in a place most people miss: the creative is the targeting now.

Because the algorithm reads your ad to decide who to show it to, the more context you give it, the better it finds your people. A short, basic ad gives it almost nothing to work with, so it can only reach a small slice. Longer copy that genuinely describes who the product is for and what problem it solves hands the algorithm a rich picture, so it can go out and find those exact buyers across a broad audience. Stop trying to out-think the targeting engineers. Go broad, and let your specific creative do the targeting. Run your old interest-stacked ad against a broad version side by side for a week and compare your cost per result. Most people are shocked.

## Six: match your ad to your page

Most advertisers pour ninety percent of their effort into the ad and almost none into the page people land on. That is a costly mistake. If someone clicks an ad and the page does not match what they just clicked, the promise, the look, the words, they lose the scent and bounce straight back off. You can have a brilliant click-through rate and cheap clicks, and still have your sales in the toilet, because the page broke the connection.

The fix is congruence, which just means the page matches the ad. Meta is the best split-testing tool on earth, because your ad headlines get shown to far more people than ever reach your page, so you learn fast which words work. Take your best performing ad headline and mirror it on your product page, in the main heading, the sub-heading, and the opening line. Doing just this often lifts conversion rate by fifteen to twenty percent. So today, line up your top ads next to their pages, and if the headlines do not match, fix it.

## Seven: retarget with a different offer

When someone visits and does not buy, the lazy move is to chase them with the same ad again, as if nagging will change their mind. There was a reason they did not buy, and the most common reason is simple: that particular offer was not right for them.

So retarget them with something different. If you sell more than one thing, show the people who did not buy a different product. You will be surprised how often someone who ignored one item buys a completely different one. Beyond that, use your retargeting to remove the reasons people hesitate. Run ads that handle the common objections head on. Run proof, real reviews and customer results, so they believe the claims. And put your other products in front of them too. People who already know you are far cheaper to convert than cold strangers, and a different offer is often all it takes.

## Eight: track the metric that actually matters

This is the one that separates people who run ads from people who run businesses. He told a story about an advertiser sitting on a great return who would not spend more, terrified that scaling would drop his return on ad spend percentage. And it does drop. But that is the wrong number to protect.

The percentage return on a single ad account is not what pays you. What pays you is net cash at the business level. So look at your blended return, all your real revenue against all your spend, and underneath that, your actual cash left over. Work out your breakeven, the most you can afford to spend to win a customer, and then scale hard toward it. If your return drops from ten times down to five times, but you go from spending a little to spending a lot, you make far more money in real rands at the end of the month. Protect the cash, not the percentage.

And do one more thing. Block out a few hours every month to look at your own numbers yourself. Not what a tool says, not what someone on your team says. You, going in and actually knowing your numbers. The confidence that gives you is rare, and confidence is what lets you scale with speed instead of fear.

## The through-line

Strip it all down and the eight hacks tell one story. Feed the machine a steady volume of native-looking creative. Name your buyer in the copy so the algorithm knows who to find. Multiply your winners instead of riding one to death. Go broad and let the creative do the targeting. Match your page to your ad so you do not waste the click. Retarget the people who did not buy with a different offer. And judge the whole thing on net cash, not on a percentage.

Here is your routine. One hour a week making fresh creative. One afternoon turning each new winner into variations and a broad test. A monthly look at your real numbers with your own eyes. Do that, and you are running ads like a business, not gambling like a hobby.
